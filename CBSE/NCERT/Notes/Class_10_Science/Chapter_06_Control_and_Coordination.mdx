# Control and Coordination

## Key Concepts

### Introduction
Control and coordination are essential for living organisms to respond appropriately to changes in their environment. In animals, these functions are performed by the nervous and endocrine systems, while in plants, they are achieved through chemical messengers called hormones.

### Animals - Nervous System
The nervous system is an organized network of nerve cells or **neurons** specialized for conducting information via electrical impulses.

**1. Structure of a Neuron:**
*   **Dendrites:** Specialized tips that detect stimuli and acquire information.
*   **Cell Body:** Where information travels as an electrical impulse.
*   **Axon:** A long fiber that carries the impulse to its end.
*   **Synapse:** The gap between two neurons where electrical impulses are converted into chemical signals for transmission.

**2. Reflex Action:**
A sudden, involuntary response to a stimulus in the environment.
*   **Reflex Arc:** The path followed by signals from input (sensory nerve) to output (motor nerve) through the spinal cord, allowing for quick action.

**3. Human Brain:**
The main coordinating center of the body.
*   **Fore-brain:** The thinking part; receives sensory impulses, interprets information, and controls voluntary muscles.
*   **Mid-brain and Hind-brain:** Control involuntary actions like heartbeat, breathing, blood pressure, and salivation (via the medulla).
*   **Cerebellum:** Part of the hind-brain responsible for precision, posture, and balance.

### Coordination in Plants
Plants lack a nervous system and muscles. They show two types of movement:
1.  **Independent of Growth:** Quick responses to stimuli, like the sensitive plant (*Mimosa pudica*) folding its leaves when touched. This occurs due to changes in water amount (swelling/shrinking) in cells.
2.  **Dependent on Growth (Tropism):** Directional movements toward or away from stimuli.
    *   **Phototropism:** Growth toward light (shoots).
    *   **Geotropism:** Response to gravity (roots grow down, shoots grow up).
    *   **Hydrotropism:** Response to water.
    *   **Chemotropism:** Response to chemicals (e.g., pollen tube growth toward ovules).

**Plant Hormones (Phytohormones):**
*   **Auxin:** Promotes growth at shoot tips and helps in phototropism.
*   **Gibberellins:** Assist in stem growth.
*   **Cytokinins:** Promote rapid cell division (found in fruits and seeds).
*   **Abscisic Acid:** Inhibits growth and causes wilting of leaves.

### Hormones in Animals
Chemical coordination in animals is carried out by the **Endocrine System**. Hormones are secreted directly into the blood and act on target organs.

**Key Endocrine Glands and Hormones:**
*   **Adrenaline (Adrenal Gland):** Prepares the body for "fight or flight" by increasing heartbeat and oxygen supply to muscles.
*   **Thyroxin (Thyroid Gland):** Regulates metabolism (requires iodine).
*   **Growth Hormone (Pituitary Gland):** Regulates body growth and development.
*   **Insulin (Pancreas):** Regulates blood sugar levels.
*   **Testosterone & Oestrogen:** Regulate changes associated with puberty in males and females respectively.

**Feedback Mechanism:**
The timing and amount of hormone release are regulated by feedback mechanisms. For example, the pancreas increases insulin production when blood sugar levels rise and reduces it when they fall.
