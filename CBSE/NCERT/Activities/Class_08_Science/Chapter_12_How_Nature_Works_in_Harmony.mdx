# Class 08 Science - How Nature Works in Harmony
## Activities

# Activity 12.1: Exploring Nearby Habitats

**Aim/Objective:** To identify the biotic and abiotic components in different types of habitats (e.g., Pond and Forest).

**Materials Required:**
- Nearby accessible habitats (pond, park, or large tree)
- Notebook and pen

**Procedure:**
1. Visit two different habitats in your surroundings.
2. List all the living organisms (biotic) and non-living things (abiotic) observed.
3. Record observations in a comparative table.

**Observation:**
- **Pond:** Fish, frogs, algae (biotic); Water, dissolved oxygen, mud (abiotic).
- **Forest:** Trees, birds, insects (biotic); Soil, sunlight, air (abiotic).

**Explanation:**
- A **habitat** is the natural environment where an organism lives and grows. The **biotic components** (living beings) depend on the **abiotic components** (physical factors like water and temperature) for their survival. 
- Different habitats offer different living conditions; for example, aquatic animals have adapted to extract oxygen from water, whereas terrestrial animals breathe air.

**Conclusion:**
- Every habitat is a complex system where living beings interact with their physical environment to survive.

# Activity 12.2: Recording Plant and Animal Populations

**Aim/Objective:** To understand the concept of population by counting specific organisms in a defined area.

**Materials Required:**
- Measuring tape/String
- 1m x 1m area in a garden

**Procedure:**
1. Mark a 1m x 1m square area in your school garden.
2. Identify four different types of organisms (plants or animals) within this area.
3. Count the total number of individuals for each type and record them.

**Observation:**
- The count might show 20 individuals of Plant A but only 5 of Plant B in the same square metre.

**Explanation:**
- A **population** is a group of individuals of the same species living in a specific area at a specific time. 
- Variations in population size depend on the availability of resources (nutrients, space) and interactions like competition or predation.

**Conclusion:**
- Habitats consist of multiple populations of different species living together.

# Activity 12.3: Role of Organisms in a Community (Fish and Seeds)

**Aim/Objective:** To investigate how the presence of one species (fish) affects others in a community (flowering plants).

**Materials Required:**
- Case study of two ponds (A with fish, B without fish)

**Procedure:**
1. Observe Pond A (with fish) and Pond B (no fish).
2. Compare the number of dragonflies and pollinators (bees/butterflies) near both.
3. Analyze why plants near Pond A produce more seeds.

**Observation:**
- Pond A (with fish) has fewer dragonflies and more bees/butterflies.
- Plants near Pond A have higher seed production.

**Explanation:**
- This demonstrates a **trophic cascade**. Fish eat dragonfly larvae. With fewer dragonflies (which are predators of adult bees and butterflies), the pollinator population increases. 
- More pollinators lead to more efficient **pollination** of nearby flowering plants, resulting in more seeds. This shows how every organism in a community is interconnected.

**Conclusion:**
- The balance of an ecosystem depends on the complex interactions between all members of the community.

# Activity 12.4: Identifying Biotic and Abiotic Interactions

**Aim/Objective:** To categorize interactions within an ecosystem into specific types.

**Materials Required:**
- Habitat illustration or field observation

**Procedure:**
1. Categorize interactions into three criteria: (1) Biotic-Abiotic, (2) Abiotic-Abiotic, and (3) Biotic-Biotic.
2. Provide examples for each (e.g., Earthworms in soil, sunlight heating water, frog eating insects).

**Observation:**
- Ecosystems are defined by constant exchange of energy and matter across all components.

**Explanation:**
- **Criterion 1 (Biotic-Abiotic):** Organisms use abiotic factors for life processes (e.g., plants using sunlight for photosynthesis).
- **Criterion 2 (Abiotic-Abiotic):** Physical factors affect each other (e.g., sunlight causing water evaporation).
- **Criterion 3 (Biotic-Biotic):** Living things interact (e.g., competition for food, or mutualism in pollination).

**Conclusion:**
- Interactions are the functional essence of an ecosystem.

# Activity 12.5: Classifying Organisms by Eating Habits

**Aim/Objective:** To categorize organisms based on their mode of nutrition in a forest ecosystem.

**Materials Required:**
- List of forest organisms (Deer, Hare, Vulture, Fox, etc.)

**Procedure:**
1. Identify the diet of each listed organism.
2. Classify them as Producers, Herbivores, Carnivores, or Omnivores.

**Observation:**
- Deer and Hare: Herbivores. Fox: Omnivore. Vulture: Carnivore (Scavenger). Trees: Producers.

**Explanation:**
- **Autotrophs (Producers)** make their own food via photosynthesis. **Heterotrophs (Consumers)** must eat other organisms. 
- This hierarchy is essential for the flow of energy through the ecosystem.

**Conclusion:**
- Diverse eating habits allow for the distribution of energy across different trophic levels.

# Activity 12.6: Constructing a Food Chain

**Aim/Objective:** To represent the feeding relationships among organisms in a linear sequence.

**Materials Required:**
- List of grassland organisms (Grass, Grasshopper, Frog, Snake, Eagle)

**Procedure:**
1. Arrange the organisms in a sequence of "who eats whom."
2. Use arrows to show the direction of energy flow.

**Observation:**
- Grass ? Grasshopper ? Frog ? Snake ? Eagle.

**Explanation:**
- A **food chain** shows the unidirectional flow of energy from producers to various levels of consumers. 
- Each step is a **trophic level**. Energy is lost as heat at each step, which is why food chains usually have only 4-5 steps.

**Conclusion:**
- Food chains illustrate the basic predatory-prey links in an ecosystem.

# Activity 12.7: Drawing a Trophic Pyramid

**Aim/Objective:** To visualize the number of organisms at each trophic level in the form of a pyramid.

**Materials Required:**
- Data from a crop field (Millet, Mouse, Eagle)

**Procedure:**
1. Count the relative numbers of each organism.
2. Arrange them in ascending order (highest number at the base).
3. Draw the resulting pyramid shape.

**Observation:**
- Millets (Base) &gt; Mice (Middle) &gt; Eagles (Top).

**Explanation:**
- This is a **Pyramid of Numbers**. Producers are always the most numerous to support the higher levels. 
- Since only about 10% of energy is passed to the next level, the total biomass and number of individuals typically decrease as you go up.

**Conclusion:**
- The pyramid structure reflects the energy constraints of an ecosystem.

# Activity 12.8: Tracing a Food Web

**Aim/Objective:** To understand that multiple food chains are interlinked in nature.

**Materials Required:**
- Diagram of various organisms

**Procedure:**
1. Identify all possible feeding links between the organisms.
2. Draw multiple overlapping arrows.

**Observation:**
- One organism (e.g., a mouse) can be eaten by both an owl and a fox.

**Explanation:**
- In nature, feeding relationships are not simple chains. A **food web** is a more realistic representation of an ecosystem's complexity. 
- It provides stability; if one prey species declines, the predator can switch to another, maintaining the balance.

**Conclusion:**
- Interlinked food chains form a resilient network called a food web.

# Activity 12.9: Case Study: Intervention in Nature (Frog Leg Export)

**Aim/Objective:** To analyze the cascading effects of human intervention on an ecosystem's balance.

**Materials Required:**
- Historical data on Indian Bullfrog export

**Procedure:**
1. Study the effect of large-scale harvesting of bullfrogs in India during the 1980s.
2. Analyze the subsequent rise in pests and pesticide use.

**Observation:**
- Decline in frogs led to an increase in agricultural pests.

**Explanation:**
- Frogs act as a natural **biological control** for insects. When they were removed, the pest population exploded. 
- Farmers then used synthetic pesticides, which caused further environmental pollution. This demonstrates the danger of disrupting natural food webs for commercial gain.

**Conclusion:**
- Removing even one species can have unpredictable and harmful consequences for the entire ecosystem.

# Activity 12.10: Survey of Sustainable Farming Practices

**Aim/Objective:** To investigate the impact of modern farming on soil health and environment.

**Materials Required:**
- Survey questionnaire for local farmers

**Procedure:**
1. Interview farmers about their use of synthetic fertilizers and pesticides.
2. Ask about changes in soil health and pest resistance over time.
3. Prepare a report on your findings.

**Observation:**
- Overuse of chemicals leads to soil degradation, loss of helpful microbes, and increased pest resistance.

**Explanation:**
- Sustainable farming focuses on **organic manures** (like Kunapa Jala) and maintaining soil biodiversity. 
- Excessive synthetic inputs destroy the **humus** and beneficial organisms (like earthworms), leading to soil erosion and loss of long-term productivity.

**Conclusion:**
- Understanding ecological principles is key to developing sustainable agricultural systems.
