# Class 06 Science - Diversity in the Living World
## Activities

# Activity 2.1: Nature Walk

**Aim/Objective:** To observe and experience the beauty and diversity of plants and animals in their natural surroundings.

**Materials Required:**
- Notebook
- Pen
- Water bottle
- Magnifying glass (optional)

**Procedure:**
1. Plan a nature walk to a nearby park, forest, or school garden.
2. Observe different types of plants (grasses, bushes, trees) and animals (insects, birds, mammals).
3. Note the features of stems, leaves, flowers, and the behavior of animals.
4. Listen to the different sounds (bird chirps) and notice the various smells in the environment.
5. Record observations in a table.

**Observation:**
- There is a vast variety of plants with different heights, stem textures, and leaf shapes.
- Animals exhibit different movements (flying, jumping, crawling) and occupy different habitats (trees, soil, water).

**Explanation:**
- Biodiversity refers to the variety of life in a particular habitat or ecosystem. Each species has unique adaptations (features) that help it survive in its environment.

**Conclusion:**
- Nature is highly diverse, and all living organisms are interconnected and dependent on each other.

# Activity 2.2: Appreciation of Nature

**Aim/Objective:** To express individual appreciation for specific plants and animals through drawing.

**Materials Required:**
- Blackboard and chalk or Paper and colors

**Procedure:**
1. Close your eyes for 30 seconds and think of one plant and one animal you find interesting.
2. Draw the chosen plant and animal.
3. Observe the variety of drawings produced by the entire class.

**Observation:**
- The class produces a wide range of drawings, reflecting the many different species students are familiar with.

**Explanation:**
- Personal experiences and observation help us appreciate the richness of biodiversity. The large number of different drawings highlights the sheer variety of life forms.

**Conclusion:**
- Individual appreciation reflects the broad spectrum of diversity in the living world.

# Activity 2.3: Grouping Organisms

**Aim/Objective:** To understand the importance of classification by grouping plants and animals based on common features.

**Materials Required:**
- Pictures of plants and animals (from magazines, newspapers, or charts)
- Cards and glue

**Procedure:**
1. Collect and paste pictures of various organisms on individual cards.
2. Divide the class into small groups.
3. Observe the features of the organisms on the cards.
4. Group them based on similarities (e.g., height, presence of flowers, habitat, or diet).
5. Discuss the criteria used for grouping.

**Observation:**
- Different groups may use different criteria for classification (e.g., color vs. size).

**Explanation:**
- Classification is the process of grouping organisms based on shared characteristics. It helps scientists organize and study millions of species systematically.

**Conclusion:**
- Grouping makes it easier to understand and study the diversity of life.

# Activity 2.4: Classification of Plants (Herbs, Shrubs, Trees)

**Aim/Objective:** To group plants based on their height, nature of stem, and branching pattern.

**Materials Required:**
- Plants in a garden or park
- Notebook

**Procedure:**
1. Observe the height of various plants relative to your own height.
2. Touch the stems to check if they are soft (tender) or hard (woody), and thin or thick.
3. Check if branches arise close to the ground or higher up on the stem.
4. Categorize them into herbs, shrubs, or trees.

**Observation:**
- Herbs: Small, green, tender stems.
- Shrubs: Medium height, woody stems branching near the base.
- Trees: Tall, thick, hard brown stems with branches higher up.

**Explanation:**
- Plants are classified based on their growth habit. This morphological classification helps in identifying and studying the life span and structural strength of different plants.

**Conclusion:**
- Plants can be systematically categorized into herbs, shrubs, and trees based on physical characteristics.

# Activity 2.5: Observing Leaf Venation

**Aim/Objective:** To compare the patterns of veins in different leaves.

**Materials Required:**
- Variety of leaves (e.g., Hibiscus, Banana, Grass, Peepal)

**Procedure:**
1. Observe the thin lines (veins) on the surface of different leaves.
2. Identify the pattern: net-like or parallel.

**Observation:**
- Some leaves have a net-like pattern (Reticulate Venation, e.g., Hibiscus, Peepal).
- Some leaves have veins running parallel to each other (Parallel Venation, e.g., Grass, Banana).

**Explanation:**
- Venation is the arrangement of veins in a leaf. It provides structural support and helps in the transport of water and nutrients. The type of venation is often linked to other plant characteristics like root types.

**Conclusion:**
- Leaf venation is a key feature used to distinguish between different groups of plants.

# Activity 2.6: Exploring Root Systems

**Aim/Objective:** To identify the two main types of root systems in plants.

**Materials Required:**
- Wild herbs or grasses
- Trowel (Khurpi)
- Water for washing roots

**Procedure:**
1. Carefully dig out a few herbs/grasses without damaging the roots.
2. Wash the soil off the roots.
3. Observe the structure: is there one main root or a bunch of thin roots?

**Observation:**
- Taproot: One thick main root with smaller side roots (e.g., Mustard, Hibiscus).
- Fibrous Root: A cluster of thin, similar-sized roots arising from the base (e.g., Grass, Wheat).

**Explanation:**
- Roots anchor the plant and absorb water. Dicot plants typically develop a taproot from the radicle, while monocot plants develop a fibrous root system where the primary root is short-lived.

**Conclusion:**
- Plants possess either a taproot or a fibrous root system.

# Activity 2.7: Relation between Venation and Roots

**Aim/Objective:** To analyze the correlation between leaf venation and the type of root system.

**Materials Required:**
- Five different saplings (e.g., Lemongrass, Marigold, Sadabahar)

**Procedure:**
1. Observe the leaf venation and the root system of each plant before planting.
2. Record the findings in a table.

**Observation:**
- Plants with reticulate venation generally have taproots (e.g., Sadabahar).
- Plants with parallel venation generally have fibrous roots (e.g., Lemongrass).

**Explanation:**
- There is a strong biological correlation between leaf and root morphology. This is because these traits are evolved together in the two major groups of flowering plants (Dicots and Monocots).

**Conclusion:**
- Leaf venation is a reliable indicator of the type of root system a plant has.

# Activity 2.8: Dicot and Monocot Seeds

**Aim/Objective:** To distinguish between plants based on the number of cotyledons in their seeds.

**Materials Required:**
- Chickpea (Chana) seeds
- Maize seeds
- Water for soaking

**Procedure:**
1. Soak seeds in water for 2-3 days.
2. Remove the seed coat and try to split the seeds.
3. Observe if they split into two parts or stay as one.

**Observation:**
- Chickpea splits into two parts (Two cotyledons - Dicot).
- Maize does not split and has one part (One cotyledon - Monocot).

**Explanation:**
- Cotyledons are the "seed leaves" that provide nutrition to the developing embryo. This fundamental difference in seed structure defines the two main classes of flowering plants: Monocots and Dicots.

**Conclusion:**
- Seeds can be classified as monocots or dicots, which correlates with their root and leaf patterns.

# Activity 2.9: Animal Movement and Body Parts

**Aim/Objective:** To identify the body parts used by different animals for movement.

**Materials Required:**
- Animal pictures or observation in nature
- Poster paper

**Procedure:**
1. List various animals (Ant, Goat, Bird, Fish, etc.).
2. Note their type of movement (walk, fly, swim, jump).
3. Identify the body parts used (legs, wings, fins).

**Observation:**
- Different animals use specialized body parts for movement suited to their environment.

**Explanation:**
- Movement (Locomotion) is essential for animals to find food, mates, and escape predators. Body parts like wings (for air), fins (for water), and legs (for land) are adaptations to their specific habitats.

**Conclusion:**
- Diversity in animal movement is a result of structural adaptations to their surroundings.

# Activity 2.10: Adaptations in Different Surroundings

**Aim/Objective:** To observe how plants and animals differ across various habitats (habitats).

**Materials Required:**
- Reference images or knowledge of different regions (Desert, Mountain, Ocean, Forest).

**Procedure:**
1. List plants and animals found in different regions (e.g., Camel in desert, Fish in ocean).
2. Compare their physical features.

**Observation:**
- Desert plants (Cactus) have fleshy stems; mountain trees (Deodar) are conical with sloping branches.
- Animals in different regions have specific features (e.g., streamlined bodies in fish, thick fur in mountain animals).

**Explanation:**
- Adaptation is the process by which organisms develop features that help them survive in a specific environment. For example, conical shapes in mountain trees help snow slide off easily.

**Conclusion:**
- The types of organisms found in a region are determined by the environmental conditions and their specific adaptations.
