## Activities

# Activity 6.1: Interdependence of Taste and Smell

**Aim/Objective:** To investigate the role of olfactory receptors in perceiving the full flavour of food.

**Materials Required:**
- Sugar
- Lunch or other flavorful food
- Fingers to block nose

**Procedure:**
1. Put some sugar in your mouth and observe the taste.
2. Block your nose by pressing it and eat sugar again. Note if there is any difference in taste.
3. While eating lunch, block your nose and notice if you can fully appreciate the taste of the food.
4. Compare the sensations of eating with an open vs. blocked nose.

**Observation:**
- There is little to no difference in the taste of plain sugar (which is sweet) whether the nose is blocked or open.
- However, for complex foods like lunch, the ability to perceive the "flavor" is significantly reduced when the nose is blocked. The food feels relatively "bland."

**Explanation:**
- Taste is perceived by **gustatory receptors** on the tongue, which detect basic tastes: sweet, salty, sour, bitter, and umami. Sugar primarily activates sweet receptors.
- "Flavour" is a combination of both taste and smell. As we chew, volatile aromatic compounds from the food travel to the **olfactory receptors** in the nasal cavity (via the back of the throat).
- When the nose is blocked, the olfactory signals are cut off, and the brain receives only the basic taste signals from the tongue. This is why food tastes "different" or bland when we have a cold or block our nose.

**Conclusion:**
- The full perception of flavour depends on the coordinated input from both gustatory and olfactory sensory systems.

# Activity 6.2: Phototropism in Growing Plants

**Aim/Objective:** To demonstrate the directional growth of plants (phototropism) in response to light.

**Materials Required:**
- Conical flask filled with water
- Wire mesh
- 2-3 freshly germinated bean seeds
- Cardboard box open from one side
- Sunlight (from a window)

**Procedure:**
1. Place bean seeds on a wire mesh over a conical flask filled with water.
2. Place the flask inside a cardboard box that is open only on one side.
3. Orient the box so the open side faces light from a window.
4. After 2-3 days, observe the direction of growth of shoots and roots.
5. Turn the flask so the shoots face away from the light and observe for a few more days.

**Observation:**
- Initially, the shoots bend towards the light source, and the roots grow away from it.
- After turning the flask, the new growth of the shoot again bends towards the light, and the new root growth turns away.

**Explanation:**
- Plants exhibit **phototropism**, which is growth in response to light.
- **Shoots are positively phototropic**, meaning they grow towards the light to maximize photosynthesis. This is controlled by the hormone **auxin**, which accumulates on the shady side of the shoot, causing those cells to elongate faster and the shoot to bend towards the light.
- **Roots are negatively phototropic**, growing away from light towards the soil (where they can find water and minerals), also influenced by gravity (**geotropism**).

**Conclusion:**
- Plants use growth-mediated movements (tropisms) to orient themselves optimally towards environmental stimuli.

# Activity 6.3: Identifying Human Endocrine Glands

**Aim/Objective:** To locate and identify the major endocrine glands in the human body.

**Materials Required:**
- Diagram of the human endocrine system (Fig. 6.7 in textbook)
- Reference books or internet resources

**Procedure:**
1. Study the provided diagram of the human body showing various glands.
2. Identify the pituitary, thyroid, adrenal, pancreas, testes (in males), and ovaries (in females).
3. Research the specific hormones each gland secretes and their general locations (e.g., neck, brain, abdomen).

**Observation:**
- Endocrine glands are located in specific parts of the body: Pituitary in the brain, Thyroid in the neck, Adrenals on top of the kidneys, Pancreas near the stomach, etc.

**Explanation:**
- The **endocrine system** consists of ductless glands that secrete chemical messengers called **hormones** directly into the bloodstream.
- Unlike the nervous system which uses electrical impulses for rapid, localized responses, the endocrine system provides a slower but more widespread and persistent form of control and coordination.

**Conclusion:**
- The endocrine system is a crucial regulatory network that maintains homeostasis and coordinates growth and development.

# Activity 6.4: Hormones and their Functions

**Aim/Objective:** To correlate specific human hormones with their producing glands and biological functions.

**Materials Required:**
- Table 6.1 (to be completed)
- Biological knowledge of the endocrine system

**Procedure:**
1. List the major hormones: Growth Hormone, Thyroxin, Insulin, Testosterone, Oestrogen, Adrenaline, and Releasing Hormones.
2. Identify the gland responsible for each.
3. Describe the primary function or effect of each hormone on the body.

**Observation/Data Table:**
1. **Growth Hormone:** Pituitary gland; Stimulates growth in all organs.
2. **Thyroxin:** Thyroid gland; Regulates carbohydrate, protein, and fat metabolism.
3. **Insulin:** Pancreas; Regulates blood sugar levels.
4. **Testosterone:** Testes; Development of male sex organs and secondary sexual characters.
5. **Oestrogen:** Ovaries; Development of female sex organs and regulation of menstrual cycle.
6. **Adrenaline:** Adrenal gland; Prepares the body for "fight or flight" (increases heart rate, etc.).
7. **Releasing Hormones:** Hypothalamus; Stimulates the pituitary gland to release other hormones.

**Explanation:**
- Hormones act as chemical signals that trigger specific responses in target organs. Their secretion is tightly regulated by **feedback mechanisms** (e.g., blood sugar levels controlling insulin).
- Imbalance in hormone levels can lead to disorders like diabetes (low insulin), goitre (iodine/thyroxin deficiency), or dwarfism/gigantism (growth hormone imbalance).

**Conclusion:**
- Hormones are essential for the metabolic, reproductive, and developmental processes of the human body.
